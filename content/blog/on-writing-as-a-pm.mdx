There's a pattern I've noticed across every strong PM I've worked with or learned from: they write. Not just PRDs and specs — they write to think.

## Writing surfaces what you don't know

When you can't explain something clearly in writing, it usually means you don't understand it clearly. A fuzzy paragraph is a signal, not a problem with your writing — it's a problem with your thinking.

Before I take any important meeting or make any significant decision, I write a short document. Not to share necessarily, but to force clarity. Often I discover mid-paragraph that I don't actually know the answer to something I thought I did.

## The PRD is a communication tool, not a deliverable

I see a lot of PMs treat the PRD as the goal. Ship the document, check the box, move on. But the document is only useful insofar as it aligns people and reduces misunderstandings.

A 20-page PRD nobody reads aligns nobody. A 2-page document that engineers, designers, and leadership have all internalized does more work.

**Write shorter. Say more.**

## Public writing compounds

Writing for an audience — even a small one — changes how you write. You can't hide behind internal jargon. You have to actually make the argument, not just assert it.

And over time, a body of writing becomes something you can point to. It shows how you think, not just what you've shipped.

If you're a PM trying to break into AI companies, a thoughtful essay about a product you use, a problem you've observed, or an idea you'd build is worth more than most certifications.
